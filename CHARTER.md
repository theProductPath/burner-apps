# Burner Apps Charter
**The Philosophy Behind the Methodology**
*The Product Path | Established 2026*

---

## Definition

A Burner App is a purpose-built, single-use application designed to solve one clearly defined problem — and then get out of the way.

It's intentionally narrow in scope, optimized for speed and effectiveness rather than longevity. It carries no expectation of long-term maintenance, evolution, or survival. When it completes its job, it gets deleted. That's not a failure condition. That's success.

**Success includes deletion.**

---

## Why "Burner"

The name matters. "Burner" captures something that more clinical names don't: the act of letting go is a feature, not a failure.

Like a burner phone — purpose-built, disposable, no strings attached. You use it for what you need, and when you're done, it's gone. No orphaned data. No broken integrations. No one asking "who's maintaining this?" six months later.

The name also signals something important to clients: *this was built specifically for you, for this moment.* That's not a limitation. That's premium.

---

## The Six Principles

### 1. Disposable by Design
The temporary nature isn't a constraint — it's the entire point. When you stop trying to build tools that last forever, you can build tools that are perfect for right now. Permanent solutions carry permanent costs. Burner Apps don't.

### 2. Success Includes Deletion
A Burner App that completes its purpose and gets deleted is a success story. The goal is outcomes, not artifacts. The deliverable — a decision made, a dataset validated, a problem understood — outlives the tool that produced it.

### 3. Scope is Sacred
One problem. One solution. No feature creep. No "while we're at it." No roadmap. The app does exactly what it was built to do and nothing more. Scope discipline is what makes Burner Apps fast and effective.

### 4. Speed Over Elegance
Burner Apps prioritize getting to done over architectural purity. Quick decisions, pragmatic shortcuts, and "good enough" solutions are virtues here. The code doesn't need to be maintainable because it won't be maintained.

### 5. Zero Friction for the Consumer
The end user opens the file and immediately knows what to do. No installation, no login, no setup, no training. If it requires explanation, it's too complicated. Note: this applies to the *Consumer*, not the Creator. The Creator runs scripts and merges data — that complexity is expected and acceptable. It stays invisible to the Consumer.

### 6. Artifacts Over Applications
What matters is what the app produces — a report generated, data validated, a decision made, a problem understood. Not the app itself. The deliverable outlives the tool.

## The Mindset Shift

Building Burner Apps requires unlearning some deep instincts. Every developer — and every product person — has been trained to think in terms of longevity. Reusable code. Extensible architecture. Future-proof design.

Burner Apps reject all of that. Not because those things are bad, but because they're expensive — and for a bounded problem with a short timeline, they're a tax you don't need to pay.

| Traditional Instinct | Burner App Mindset |
|---|---|
| "What if we need to add features later?" | We won't. Build exactly what's needed now. |
| "This code should be reusable." | This code should be deletable. |
| "Let's make it configurable." | Let's make it obvious. |
| "We need proper architecture." | We need to ship today. |
| "What about edge cases?" | What about the deadline? |
| "This should be in version control." | This should be in someone's hands. |

## When to Reach for a Burner App

**The right conditions:**
- The problem is bounded — you can clearly define "done"
- The timeline is short — days or weeks, not months
- The audience is known — specific people with a specific need
- The data is a snapshot — point-in-time analysis, not live dashboards
- Feedback needs to be collected — stakeholders review, validate, or respond
- Traditional tools are overkill — Excel is too raw, a full app is too much
- You need to move fast — no time for procurement cycles or sprint planning

**The wrong conditions:**
- The problem requires ongoing operation
- Multiple users need to collaborate simultaneously
- Real-time data is essential
- The tool needs to evolve with changing requirements
- Compliance or audit trails require permanent systems
- Enterprise infrastructure integration is required

## The Three Roles

### The Creator
The technical person who builds, assembles, and delivers the Burner App. Accepts complexity in exchange for power — running Python scripts, configuring field mappings, testing outputs. Focuses on building the tool, not managing stakeholder relationships.

### The Coordinator
The project lead who bridges technical capability and business need. Defines the job, gathers the data, distributes the app, collects reports, and ensures the right people have the right tool at the right time. The Coordinator makes the Burner App feel professional and managed from the client's perspective.

### The Consumer
The end user. Opens the file, does the work, exports a report. Never sees the build process. Never needs training. Their experience is entirely simple — the Creator's complexity is invisible to them.

This separation is what makes Burner Apps scale. Complexity is absorbed at the Creator level so it never touches the Consumer.

## The Feedback Loop

A Burner App is a round-trip system, not just a viewer. Every Burner App produces a structured report containing:

- Human-readable summary: tables, issue descriptions, notes
- Machine-readable JSON: full record data, session analytics, integrity fingerprints

The fingerprints verify the report came from the exact dataset distributed. The session analytics reveal how the Consumer engaged. The Creator processes programmatically. The Coordinator communicates results. The circle closes cleanly — without email chains, follow-up meetings, or inconsistent feedback formats.

---

## The Power of Bespoke

When you hand a client a Burner App, you're not handing them a generic tool they have to adapt. You're handing them something that contains their data, their record types, their terminology, their task. The welcome screen tells them exactly what to do. The interface reflects how they think about their information.

"This was custom-generated for your review" is a sentence that changes how people experience software. It transforms temporary into premium. Disposable into thoughtful.

That reframe is available to every Burner App — because every one of them *was* custom-generated. That's not marketing. It's architecture.

---

## A Note on Simplicity vs. Complexity

The simplicity of the Consumer experience is not accidental. It's the result of deliberate complexity absorbed by the Creator and Coordinator.

The generator script is not simple. Configuring field mappings, relationship definitions, welcome text, and expiration windows takes judgment and iteration. The report processor handles fingerprint verification and cross-referencing that requires real engineering.

None of that is visible to the Consumer. They open a file, do their job, and leave. The sophistication is in making that possible — not in showing them the mechanism.
